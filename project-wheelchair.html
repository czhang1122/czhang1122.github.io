Canine Rehabilitation
Wheelchair

A custom mobility aid for dogs with hind-limb paralysis — designed from scratch, fabricated, and iterated with real veterinary input to restore locomotive independence.

Context Graduate Design Project
Institution Northeastern University
Role Lead Designer
Tools SolidWorks · FEA · Manual Fab

The problem

Dogs with degenerative myelopathy, intervertebral disc disease, or spinal injury progressively lose use of their hind limbs. Without intervention, they drag their rear quarters along the ground — abrading skin, developing sores, and losing the ability to exercise and relieve themselves independently. Off-the-shelf wheelchairs exist, but they're expensive, ship in fixed sizes, and often fit poorly for non-standard breeds.

The goal: design a wheelchair that could be built and adjusted to fit any medium-to-large dog without requiring breed-specific custom fabrication for every animal.

Design approach

The frame was designed in SolidWorks around three independently adjustable dimensions: torso length, chassis width (hip span), and ride height. Each axis uses a slip-tube-and-clamp configuration — no tools needed for fit adjustment, so a veterinary technician or owner can re-fit the device between sessions.

Material selection balanced weight against stiffness. The primary structure uses aluminum tubing for the main rails — light, corrosion-resistant, and easy to cut to custom lengths. Cross-members at the saddle and rear wheel axle are steel for strength at the load points.

"The hardest constraint wasn't structural — it was making something a dog owner could adjust in a parking lot, in the rain, without a degree in mechanical engineering."

Structural validation

Before committing to final material choices and wall thicknesses, the assembly was subjected to finite element analysis with loading conditions derived from estimated dog mass (target: 15–35 kg) and dynamic gait forces (applied as a multiplier on static weight). The critical load case was a lateral stumble — asymmetric loading at the rear axle while the dog pivots.

FEA identified stress concentrations at the saddle-to-main-rail junction. The solution was a gusset plate and a slightly larger-diameter cross-tube — adding minimal weight while dropping peak von Mises stress well below the aluminum yield limit with a 2.5× safety factor.

Harness & fit system

The saddle is a padded neoprene sling that spans the dog's pelvis, distributing load across the hip bones rather than concentrating it at a single point. Load path analysis showed that a trapezoidal sling geometry reduced peak pressure on any single contact point by approximately 40% compared to a flat-strap design.

Front attachment uses a chest harness (owner-supplied) with a quick-release carabiner interface — the dog walks in from the front, clips in, and rolls. No lifting the full animal weight to thread through a frame.

Iteration & testing

The project ran through three distinct prototype iterations, each tested with input from a veterinary professional:

  • Prototype 1: Established basic geometry and adjustability. Identified saddle too far aft — caused the dog to lean forward and overload forelimbs. Fixed in P2 by moving saddle mount 40 mm forward.
  • Prototype 2: Corrected saddle position. Identified wheel camber issue — rear wheels toed in under load, causing lateral drift. Resolved with revised axle geometry and positive camber.
  • Prototype 3: Final geometry. Added foam grip wrap to adjustment tubes and zip-tie anchor points for harness routing. Passed veterinary sign-off for extended use.
