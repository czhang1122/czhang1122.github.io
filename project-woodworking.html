Why woodworking belongs on an engineering portfolio

Woodworking is tolerance work without a CNC machine. Joinery that fits cleanly requires the same spatial reasoning as machined interfaces — you're working by hand to a layout line instead of a CAD datum, but the underlying geometry is identical. Dovetails are just prismatic mating features with a draft angle. Mortise-and-tenon joints are a shoulder-and-tab interface with a glue-line tolerance to manage.

The material adds a layer that engineered stock doesn't have: grain direction, seasonal movement, species-specific hardness and splitting behavior. Getting a joint to fit cleanly across a large glue-up requires understanding how the wood will move — a 12-inch wide panel can expand 1/4" seasonally in New England. That has to be designed in, not discovered after the finish goes on.

Every build starts with a plan, requires sequencing operations correctly before committing irreversible cuts, and ends with a fit check. The craft sharpens fabrication intuition that shows up in how I think about DFM and tolerance stackup on machined parts.

"The difference between a joint that fits and one that doesn't is usually the same 0.010" that separates a good machined fit from a bad one. The hand just has to do what the machine does."

What these builds develop

  • Fabrication intuition — knowing before a cut whether the part will fit; reading material behavior from surface texture and grain; anticipating where stress will concentrate in a joint
  • Operation sequencing — you can't machine a datum face after you've already cut the joinery that references it. The planning discipline is identical to process planning for machined parts
  • Material selection judgment — hardness, stability, availability, cost, workability. The same axes that matter when choosing stock material for a mechanical component
  • Finishing and surface quality — understanding how a final surface communicates quality in both woodwork and engineered hardware; what causes tearout, how to prevent it
  • Shop safety and tool discipline — consistent habits around guarding, workholding, and situational awareness around moving cutters; directly transferable to machine shop environments
