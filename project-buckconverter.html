Background

DC–DC buck converters are everywhere in electromechanical systems — motor drives, embedded sensor boards, EV battery management units. Getting the feedback control right is the difference between a stable regulated output and one that oscillates or drifts under load. This project built the full design pipeline from physics to verified simulation.

Small-signal modeling

The starting point is the averaged model. Rather than simulating individual switching events, state-space averaging replaces the switch with its duty-cycle-weighted average behavior, producing a continuous-time linear model that captures the converter's dynamics around a chosen operating point.

For the buck converter, the two state variables are inductor current iL and capacitor voltage vC. Perturbing around the DC operating point and dropping second-order terms gives the small-signal duty-cycle-to-output transfer function:

G(s) = Vin · (1 + s·ESR·C) / (1 + s·(L/R + ESR·C) + s²·LC)

The LC double-pole dominates the phase response — at resonance, phase drops sharply toward −180°. The ESR zero from capacitor series resistance partially recovers phase at higher frequencies, but its location depends on the specific capacitor selected.

Controller design

The open-loop plant has insufficient phase margin at reasonable crossover frequencies — the LC resonance pulls phase below −135° before any useful crossover can occur. A PI compensator was selected to add integral action (zero steady-state error to DC load steps) while placing the compensator zero to recover phase near the target crossover frequency.

Design procedure:

  • Select target crossover frequency fc well below the switching frequency (typically fsw/5 to fsw/10) to keep the averaged model valid
  • Evaluate |G(jω)| and ∠G(jω) at fc — determine required gain adjustment and phase boost needed
  • Place PI zero one decade below fc to ensure maximum phase contribution at crossover
  • Set proportional gain Kp so |G(jωc) · C(jωc)| = 1 (0 dB) at the target crossover
  • Verify closed-loop phase margin in MATLAB; iterate zero placement if margin falls below 45°

Verification in LTSpice

The designed compensator was implemented in LTSpice using a behavioral voltage source to represent the PI transfer function. Three test conditions verified the design:

  • Step response: 50% load step — output voltage recovers to setpoint within the predicted settling time with <5% overshoot
  • Line rejection: ±10% input voltage step — output deviation stays within ±50 mV
  • ESR sensitivity: Capacitor ESR varied ±50% from nominal — phase margin stays above 40° across the range

Hardware lab

The course also included a hardware buck converter lab where the control design was implemented on a physical board and verified with an oscilloscope. Switching waveforms, inductor current ripple, and output voltage ripple were measured and compared against analytical predictions.
