Ball Launcher

The brief

Solo soccer training is limited by the need for a second person to pass or cross the ball. A consistent, programmable launcher removes that dependency — enabling a single player to drill receiving, shooting off crosses, or first-touch repetitions at any time. The system needed to deliver balls repeatably at controlled speed, angle, and spin with minimal setup time.

System architecture

The launcher is organized into four subsystems, each designed and validated independently before integration:

  • Drive mechanism: Dual counter-rotating flywheels mounted on a common pitch axis. Each wheel is driven by an independent brushless DC motor — differential speed between the two wheels applies top or backspin to the ball at launch.
  • Pitch adjustment: Servo-actuated tilt mechanism sets launch angle from 5° (ground pass) to 45° (lofted cross). Angle is set via serial command before each drill sequence.
  • Feed system: Gravity-fed hopper holds up to 8 balls. A servo-driven gate at the hopper exit releases one ball at a time on command, with a 1.5 s minimum inter-ball interval to allow flywheels to return to set speed.
  • Controller: Arduino Mega with quadrature encoder feedback on each flywheel motor. A PI loop regulates wheel speed to the target RPM within ±3% before allowing ball release.

Flywheel mechanism design

The flywheel gap was the critical geometric parameter — too wide and the ball slips through without enough compression force to accelerate; too narrow and the motors stall under the nip load. Using a nip-force model derived from the ball's known compression stiffness (measured with a simple bench fixture), the gap was set to produce approximately 18 N of nip force at the nominal ball diameter, giving sufficient friction for launch while keeping motor current within the continuous rating.

Flywheel mass was sized to store enough rotational kinetic energy that a single ball pass drops wheel speed by less than 5% — keeping consistent exit velocity across the full hopper load without waiting for full speed recovery between shots.

"Every subsystem works on the bench. Integration is where the real engineering happens — the flywheels, the feeder, and the controller all have to agree on timing."

Controls & electronics

Each motor is driven by a BLDC ESC receiving PWM from the Arduino. Encoder counts are read via hardware interrupt pins to avoid timing jitter. The PI controller runs at 100 Hz; proportional gain was tuned on the bench to minimize overshoot on speed step commands, with integral gain set to eliminate steady-state error within 0.5 s.

The user interface is a simple serial terminal (115200 baud) — commands set target RPM for each wheel, pitch angle, number of balls, and inter-shot interval. A status line streams back actual wheel RPM and gate state at 10 Hz so the operator can confirm the system is ready before stepping back.

Field testing & results

Final validation was conducted on a full-size soccer pitch. Key measurements:

  • Launch angle repeatability: ±5° across 20 consecutive shots at the same pitch setting
  • Exit velocity range: 20–65 km/h (ground pass to hard cross) controlled by flywheel RPM setpoint
  • Spin control: three modes (neutral, topspin at +15% differential, backspin at −15% differential) consistently produced measurable ball curve in flight
  • 8-ball sequence time: approximately 24 seconds from first shot to last at 3-second intervals
